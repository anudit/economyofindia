De-Salination — Four Core Challenges

Physics floor 1.06 kWh/m³  →  Best plant today 2.43 kWh/m³  →  Older plants 4.5+ kWh/m³

The gap between the physics floor and real plants comes from four compounding losses.

Challenge 1 Osmotic Pressure

Salt in water creates a natural pressure that resists flow through the membrane. You must pump harder than this pressure just to move any water across — and seawater requires ~30 bar before a single drop passes.

Van 't Hoff Law

$$\Pi = i \cdot C \cdot R \cdot T$$

Π — osmotic pressure (bar)

i — ion count per molecule (NaCl = 2)

C — salt concentration (mol/m³)

R, T — gas constant & temperature (K)

Seawater: $$\Pi \approx 29.7\ \text{bar}$$

Can it be beaten?

Partially — better membranes reduce overpressure needed above the osmotic floor.

Gap cost

+0.3–1.0 kWh/m³

Challenge 2 The Thermodynamic Floor

Separating salt from water requires energy by the laws of thermodynamics — even a perfect machine with zero friction cannot go below this limit. It sets a hard minimum that no desalination process can ever undercut.

Gibbs Free Energy

$$W_{\min} = -\Pi_0 \cdot \ln(1 - Y)$$

Wmin — minimum energy to separate (kWh/m³)

Π₀ — feed osmotic pressure (~0.82 kWh/m³ equiv.)

Y — water recovery fraction (e.g. 0.5 = 50%)

At 50% recovery: $$SEC_{\min} \approx 1.06\ \text{kWh/m}^3$$

Can it be beaten?

No — it is a law of physics. The goal is to approach it, not cross it.

Best plant today

2.43 kWh/m³

2.3× the floor

Challenge 3 The Recovery Penalty

As you extract more fresh water, the remaining brine becomes saltier — raising its osmotic pressure. The pump must work harder for each additional litre. Push recovery above ~50% and energy costs rise sharply.

Logarithmic Penalty

$$SEC_{\min}(Y) = \frac{-\Pi_0}{Y} \cdot \ln(1 - Y)$$

SECmin — minimum specific energy at recovery Y

Π₀ — initial feed osmotic pressure

Y — fraction of feed converted to fresh water

50% recovery: 1.14  |  80% recovery: 1.66 kWh/m³

Can it be beaten?

Partially — Batch RO tracks pressure dynamically; 40–50% recovery is the current sweet spot.

Gap cost

+0.3–0.6 kWh/m³

Challenge 4 Energy Recovery & Pump Losses

High-pressure brine exits the membrane still carrying most of its pressure energy. Without an energy recovery device (ERD), that energy is wasted as heat. Pump inefficiency adds more loss on top — together this is the single biggest gap driver.

Actual Plant SEC

$$SEC = \frac{\Pi_0}{Y \cdot \eta_{\text{pump}} \cdot \eta_{\text{ERD}}} \cdot \ln\!\left(\frac{1}{1-Y}\right) + SEC_{\text{pre}}$$

ηpump — pump efficiency (best ~0.92; older ~0.80)

ηERD — energy recovery efficiency (isobaric ~0.96; none = 1.0 i.e. no recovery)

SECpre — pretreatment energy (filtration, chemicals: ~0.2–0.5 kWh/m³)

No ERD: 5–7 kWh/m³ Isobaric ERD: 2.43 kWh/m³

Can it be beaten?

Yes — isobaric ERDs (96% efficiency) already recover most brine pressure. Further gains are marginal.

Gap cost

+1.4–3.5 kWh/m³

largest single factor
